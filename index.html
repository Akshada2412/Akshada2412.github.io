The story behind the work

I started as a mechanical engineer — trained to find where systems break and rebuild them stronger. That problem-solving lens never left me, even when I moved into software.

At Siemens, I shipped production code, ran sprints, managed cross-team dependencies. And I learned something important: the hardest engineering problem isn't technical. It's getting fifteen people to move in the same direction.

I was in the room when business decisions were made. But I was the third person watching — not the one driving. That gap, between understanding execution and owning outcomes, is exactly what brought me to UMass Amherst.

Now I build systems that don't just surface data — they tell you what to do next. A supply chain risk engine. A churn prediction model. A technical program dashboard that says: here's why the launch is at risk, here's what to do.

That's the work. Analytical enough to find the insight. Strategic enough to deliver it.
